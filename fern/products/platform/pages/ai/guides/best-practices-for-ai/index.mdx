A reliable AI agent splits the work: the prompt runs the conversation, and your code runs the business.
The practices below follow from that split — they're what we've learned makes the difference between
an agent that demos well and one you can put in front of customers.

## Split the work between the prompt and your code

The single highest-leverage design decision is what you *don't* put in the prompt.

Language models are excellent at conversation: tone, comprehension, extracting details from natural speech.
They are unreliable at arithmetic, current facts, and rule-following under pressure —
a prompt is a suggestion, not a constraint.
So give each side the work it's good at:

- **In the prompt**: who the agent is, how it speaks, what it's there to accomplish,
  and when to reach for its tools.
- **In your code, behind [tool calls][tool-calling]**: prices, inventory, calculations, policy,
  and anything else with a right answer.

A useful rule of thumb: **if the answer must be exact tomorrow, it can't live in the prompt.**

## Write the prompt for conversation, not logic

With business logic out of the way, the prompt's job is focused: define the agent's identity,
its conversational duties, and its boundaries.

- **Keep it brief.** Overloading the AI with instructions muddles its behavior —
  excessive constraints degrade performance rather than tightening it.
  See [avoiding over-prompting][prompt-eng-best-practices] for techniques.
- **Stick to necessities.** Clearly outline the AI's identity and primary responsibilities
  so it understands its role and scope.
- **Structure with Markdown.** Headings and lists keep the prompt organized and legible,
  narrowing how the AI interprets each section.

```yaml
sections:
  main:
    - ai:
        prompt:
          text: |
            Your name is David. You are a virtual assistant for a tree-planting nonprofit.

            ## Personality and duties
            You have a friendly and eco-conscious personality.
            Help callers with questions about tree planting, using the functions
            available to you to look up specific information.

            ## Greeting rules
            Greet the caller, thank them for their interest in tree planting, and
            introduce yourself as David. Prefix the greeting with 'good morning',
            'good afternoon', or 'good evening' depending on the time of day.
          temperature: 0.89
          top_p: 0.64
          confidence: 0.5
```

Notice what this prompt *doesn't* contain: no encyclopedia of tree facts, no lists of species per region.
That information has a home — the backend behind the agent's functions —
so the prompt stays short and the answers stay accurate.

For the full craft of prompt writing — structure, examples, iterative refinement —
see the [prompt engineering guide][prompt-engineering].

### Tune the model parameters

The [`prompt` object][ai-prompt-reference] accepts parameters that shape the AI's responses:

- **Temperature (`temperature`)**: influences the randomness of the AI's output.
  Values closer to 0 make responses more deterministic and focused; higher values introduce variety.
- **Top P (`top_p`)**: also controls diversity — a lower value narrows the range of potential responses.
- **Confidence (`confidence`)**: the threshold for speech-detection events.
  A lower value shortens the pause after the caller speaks, but may cause false positives.
- **Presence penalty (`presence_penalty`)**: positive values make the AI more likely to introduce new topics.
- **Frequency penalty (`frequency_penalty`)**: positive values discourage repetition.

## Keep business data out of the prompt

It's tempting to paste the catalog, the price list, or the policy manual into the prompt.
Resist it — for reasons beyond prompt length:

- **Freshness.** The prompt is written once; your data changes daily.
  A tool call reads the current value at the moment the caller asks.
- **Consistency.** Data in the prompt is *recalled* by the model, approximately.
  Data from a tool call is *returned* by your code, exactly.
- **Authority.** The model has no way to distinguish your pasted policy from its training data.
  Your backend does — it's the source of truth.

(Shorter prompts also cost fewer tokens per turn — a nice side effect, not the reason.)

Move that data behind [tool calls][tool-calling]: a lookup function backed by your webhook or the Agents SDK,
or a serverless [DataMap][swml-datamap] for straightforward API calls.

Some context genuinely belongs in the prompt — stable facts like business hours or the agent's scope.
Place dynamic or per-call values (like data interpolated into the prompt at request time)
at the **bottom** of the prompt: prompt prefixes that stay identical across turns cache better,
improving speed and consistency of responses.
For background, see [OpenAI's prompt-caching documentation](https://platform.openai.com/docs/guides/prompt-caching).

## Design for real-time voice

Voice AI adds a dimension that text chat doesn't have: time.
Two concepts matter when tuning how your agent converses:

- **End-pointing** (end-of-utterance detection): the AI's ability to detect when the caller has finished speaking.
- **Turnaround** (turn-taking): the time it takes the AI to process input and begin its response.

Instant end-pointing and turnaround is an unrealistic expectation:

- Natural language processing takes time — the AI analyzes syntax, semantics, and context, even if only for a fraction of a second.
- Real conversation is full of pauses, overlaps, and changes of direction that make "finished speaking" genuinely ambiguous.
- Generating a coherent, contextually appropriate response is computational work.

<Warning>
A sharp increase in interest in AI products has led to a number of new products claiming to offer instantaneous end-pointing and turnaround.
If an AI agent appears to respond perfectly and immediately in a recorded demo, it may be too good to be true.
</Warning>

Rather than chasing zero, make the wait feel natural:

- **Filler phrases** give the AI something to say while a function call is running —
  configure them per-function and agent-wide.
- [**Background audio**](/docs/swml/guides) such as typing or office ambience provides immediate,
  familiar feedback while processing happens.
- **Keep tool handlers fast.** Your webhook's latency is part of the conversation —
  move slow work out of band and respond promptly.

## Help speech recognition with hints

Hints boost recognition accuracy for the words that matter in your domain,
so "sapling" doesn't arrive as "sampling."

Introduce the vocabulary your agent should expect — product names, jargon, place names:

```yaml
hints:
  - wildlife
  - carbon
  - sapling
  - soil
  - watering
  - benefits
  - environment
```

In the Agents SDK, see [speech recognition hints][sdk-hints].

## Test, monitor, and iterate

- **Test with real users before deploying.** Scripted test calls follow the script;
  customers won't. Beta callers surface the gaps.
- **Review real calls.** Configure a [`post_prompt_url`][ai-reference] to receive a post-call report —
  summary, conversation log, and every function call — and read the transcripts where the agent
  hesitated or chose the wrong tool. That's where the next prompt or tool-description fix comes from.
- **Monitor usage metrics.** Token consumption, interaction times, and call outcomes
  reveal both cost and quality trends.
- **Review and update regularly.** Models, platform features, and your business all change;
  revisit the agent's prompt and tools periodically.

## Stay compliant with regulations

The FCC has ruled that calls using AI-generated voices fall under the
[Telephone Consumer Protection Act (TCPA)](https://www.fcc.gov/sites/default/files/tcpa-rules.pdf):
**unsolicited** robocalls made with AI voices are illegal without the receiver's prior consent.

This does **not** make AI agents illegal — it means they must be used responsibly and transparently:

- **Transparency**: clearly state that the caller is interacting with an AI agent.
- **Opt-out mechanism**: provide a simple, immediate way to leave the AI interaction.
- **User consent**: for outbound use especially, ensure recipients have consented to interact with an AI.
